When a sourcing director at one of Prato's largest weaving groups forwarded a spreadsheet to two counterparts "just to compare notes," she did not expect it to be sitting in eleven inboxes by the following Monday. Three mills, two trading houses and at least one freight forwarder now appear to be working from versions of the same file.

How the Spreadsheet Surfaced

The document began as an internal exercise: a single tab mapping landed cost per SKU under the old duty schedule against the new one, broken out by country of origin. Within days, colleagues at neighbouring mills had added their own rows. What started as a comparison tool became something closer to a shared early-warning system.

None of the three mills involved would speak on the record, citing ongoing contract renegotiations — but two confirmed, independently, that the underlying numbers matched what their own finance teams had modelled internally.

"We're not colluding on price. We're just no longer willing to be the only ones who didn't see this coming." — A sourcing director quoted on condition of anonymity

What's Actually Inside It

The sheet tracks four variables per SKU: base fabric cost, freight, the applicable duty rate under three different routing scenarios, and a "days to re-route" estimate. That last column is the one people keep adding to.

The Three Mills Involved

Two are based in the Prato district; the third operates a finishing facility further north. All three supply finished fabric to mid-tier and luxury accounts in Northern Europe, which makes the routing question more than academic: a two-week delay at the wrong border crossing can mean missing a delivery window entirely.

Why Sourcing Directors Are Worried

The anxiety isn't really about the tariff rate itself — most of the increases are within a band that finance teams had already modelled. It's the unpredictability of enforcement timing that's driving the spreadsheet's spread. Nobody wants to be the last to know which routing scenario actually held.

What Happens Next

Expect more of this. Once one informal cost-sharing network exists in a district as dense as Prato's, the marginal cost of joining a second one is close to zero. The Market Intelligence panel on the right tracks the inputs this particular spreadsheet leans on most heavily — cotton, energy, and EUR/USD — for exactly this reason.
